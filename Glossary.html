Amortization

Spreading an intangible asset's value throughout its course. Intangible can be applied to copyrights, trademarks, or patents.

Assets

Items you can use to further give you value. There are many different types of assets such as:
Tangible Assets, which represent physical items like stocks, land, machinery, and anything that is physically present.
Intangible Assets, which represent nonphysical terms like copyrights, trademarks, and brands.
Current Assets, which are assets that can be exchanged for cash within a certain amount of time.
Fixed Assets, can't be exchanged for cash, but they are physical items that are owned and used to generate cash.

Asset Allocation

A technique where you spread cash across many investments and their classes, such as bonds, stocks, or outright money.

Balance Sheet

An equation statement where it shows the financial worth of a company.
The equation is:
Assets (Cash) = Liabilities + Equity

Beneficiary

A person or entity receiving benefits.

Benefits

Receiving assets from another party that can be used with a particular service, such as grocery and medical services.

Bonds

Investments made in debt, where individuals let entities borrow their money to be paid back in full or interested payments.

Capital Gain

The increase in value an asset has that's larger than the price that was initially paid for.

Capital Loss

The decrease in value an asset has that's larger than the price that was initially paid for.

Capital Market

A place where investors participate in trading with assets.
There are a lot of participants in the market, including:
Companies, who sells borrowed funds and stocks.
Mutual funds, where an investor handles the investments of many individuals.
Hedge funds, an investor that hedges, lowering risk by buying a stock and lowering the value of a similar stock.

Cash Flow

The transactions of cash moving in and out of a company, either from business operations, investments, or billing/finances.

Cash Flow Statement

The statement of the company's cash transactions at a point in time, recording the spending and gaining of cash.

Compound Interest

The gaining of money that is gained when depositing and keeping the money deposited over time.

Credit

Contracted amount of money a borrower can take from a lender that has to be paid later.

Depreciation

The decrease of an asset's value due to either not being used often or the overuse of it.

Deductible

The expense a person must pay for insurance before the insurance claim can cover the costs.

Down Payment

It is the initial payment made through a credit purchase.

EBITDA

An acronym that means "Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization".
It is commonly used to estimate a company's cash flow.

Equity

The value that belongs to owners after all liabilities and assets are accounted for.

Exchange Rate

The comparison of the value of money in one country to another country's.
An example would be comparing the USA's USD to Europe's Euros or Japan's Yen.

Income Statement

Dispalys the incomes and expenses of the business during a period of time.

Liabilities

An amount of money or assets that you owe others for, which can come in different forms:
Current Liabilities: Expenses that are due soon.
Long-term Liabilities: Expenses that are paid for over a period of time.

Liquity

How quickly an asset can be sold for cash.

Loan

Cash or an item that one entity borrows from another that needs to be repaid at some point or the amount that needs to be returned will increase.

Net Worth

Overall state of financial health, by subtracting your asset value by what you owe.

Profit Margin

Profitability from the income divided by the revenue generated or sales. This can be broken down into two types:
Gross Profit Margin: Profitability coming from a product or series of products.
Net Profit Margin: Value/profitability of the entire company.

Return on Investment

The expected value of a project/activity when compared to the cost of investment, is usually represented by a percentage.
To get the percentage, the equation is:
Return on Investment = [(Income - Cost) / Cost ] * 100

Valuation

Evaluating the present worth of an asset and/or company. This is used to decide whether to sell or merge companies or get outside investments.

Working Capital

The difference coming from a company's present assets and liabilities.
